Master Thesis

November 2025 - July 2026

Circulation patterns and cloud-cover fingerprints in aquaplanet atmospheres.

Abstract

Context: The discovery of thousands of exoplanets over the past three decades has revealed an extraordinary diversity of orbital and physical characteristics, suggesting that their atmospheres exhibit climates fundamentally different from those observed on Earth or within the Solar System.

Aim: We aim to map the presence and latitudinal extent of direct and indirect atmospheric circulation cells across the parameter space of rotation rate and obliquity, determining their impact on global climatological fields and identifying distinct observational signatures.

Method: We employ ExoSPEEDY, a three-dimensional general circulation model coupled to a slab ocean with thermal relaxation towards a prescribed climatological temperature field on an aquaplanet configuration. We developed an iterative algorithm to simulate these configurations under diverse planetary parameters without introducing initial biases. Using this methodology, we diagnosed the presence of the main structures of planetary atmospheres, namely Hadley cells, Ferrel cells, and jet streams, by determining their signal-to-noise ratio, and categorized the resulting circulation regimes.

Result: Our simulations reproduced the transition in atmospheric circulation regimes for Earth-like planets under varying rotation rates, marked by the disappearance of indirect circulation cells and more efficient poleward heat transport at lower rotation rates. For variations in obliquity, the enhanced seasonality drives the formation of a global direct overturning cell. Crucially, we find that these high-obliquity regimes develop contrasting cloud-cover patterns that are determined entirely by the planetary rotation rate.

Conclusion: The methods developed in this work provide a framework for executing slab ocean-coupled climate simulations with prescribed temperatures across varied planetary settings. These results demonstrate how direct and indirect circulation cells fundamentally shape atmospheric thermal profiles. Ultimately, the resulting hemispheric asymmetries in cloud cover at high obliquities provide a practical characterization pathway to constrain rotation rate from future exoplanet observations.
